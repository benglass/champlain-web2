Course Objectives

This course is designed to research, explore, and gain competency in the complex and fascinating world of Web Design. Every web project has an end product and knowing the steps and details of the technical process of web production is essential to every professional designer. We will cover Idea and Concepting, Creative Production, Production, and Logistics of Web Design.

Student Outcomes

In addition to gaining a broad understanding of the complex processes of web, proper planning, technical details, and the potential pitfalls in the process, the student will develop his/her ability to prepare graphics and files for maximum quality outcomes whilst minimizing the cost of production, and be able to:

  • Define the steps of professional web workflow for various devices, platforms and publishing techniques.
  • Review special design considerations when working with digital images, image editing and layout and how those design choices effect the web development process.
  • Navigate the hardware and software used in the web design process, including platform requirements and pre-flight procedures.
  • Execute several representitive design projects; both in the conceptualization process and in the technical execution according to conventional professional web techniques.
  • Apply knowledge of production techniques to create web ready artwork using HTML, CSS, Javascript, and jQuery
  • Review the legal aspects of Copyright, Fair Use, Licensing Photography, Using Type Fonts.

Assessment

Each student will a series of mini projects throughout the semester focusing on specific areas of the course material such as typography and layout. These mini projects will include homework as well as in-class studio time. The final project will begin at the mid-semester point. Evaluation will be accomplished by means of the instructor grading projects based on project descriptions provided and will include written or oral critical responses to the student's project work.

Evaluation

Student project work is graded according to project description sheets, which delineate the aspects of the project (correlated to the College and/or Program Competencies,) and describe in clear language the performance criteria required in order to achieve a particular grade.

  • Homework Assignments: 10% of the total grade
  • Mini Projects: 40% of the total grade
  • Final Project: 40% of the total grade
  • Paticipation: 10% of the total grade

Course Policies

Absence/Tardiness

Absence is excused only for valid medical or personal reasons that have been properly documented and communicated to the instructor, either prior to, or a soon after the event(s) as is reasonably possible. Regardless of the reason, it is the student’s responsibility to obtain missed materials and information, to complete all assignments and/or exam(s), or obtain a valid extension. Late arrival to a class session is disruptive, both to the teacher and to the other students, and reflects an unprofessional attitude. Please do not bring food into the classroom or computer labs; beverages are allowed but must be in a closable container. Attend to your restroom needs prior to the class session.

Conduct

Students are required to conduct themselves in the classroom, studios, and labs in a respectful and constructive manner, and consistent with the guidelines provided in The Rudder. Additionally, cell phones and pagers must be turned off, instant-messaging, personal entertainment headsets, and non-course-related web surfing is strictly prohibited during class sessions. Performance regarding any of these items may inform the grading process at the instructor’s discretion.

Non-Completion

An extension for assignment work will be granted only for extenuating circumstances such as illness or serious personal problems. An extension can be obtained by submitting to the instructor properly authorized written documentation (e.g. note signed by doctor, or parent/ guardian, etc.) A grade of “Incomplete” can be obtained ONLY upon the request by the student and with the instructor’s consent, and will be issued only for substantial and clearly documented causes. Please refer to The Rudder for guidelines concerning withdrawal from a course.

Academic Honesty

Please refer to the Academic Honesty policy as outlined in The Rudder.

Special Needs

The Coordinator has specific guidelines on providing appropriate support services to students with various types of disabilities. Competencies (College-wide & GDD Program specific)

1 Aesthetic Fluency

This competency area includes the ability both to apply aesthetic principles to created works that evoke both emotional and intellectual responses in viewers, and to evaluate artistic works with regard to their aesthetic content within an historical and societal context. (Program)

2 Creativity

Creativity is the degree to which original thinking is evident in student work. Is the work very unique, and clearly the result of inventive thinking? Does the work reflect a very personal vision, and something totally unpredictable, even revelatory? (Program)

3 Critical & Creative Thinking

Critical thinking enables students to examine a problem, a question or a situation, integrate all the available information about it, arrive at a solution or hypothesis, and justify their position. More formally, critical thinking includes a wide range of cognitive skills and intellectual dispositions needed to identify, analyze, and evaluate arguments and claims, to discover and overcome personal prejudices and biases, and to formulate and present rationally convincing reasons in support of conclusions. Critical thinking skills support creativity and problem-solving. (College)

4 Discipline

[Had been Professionalism.] The degree to which, and the consistency with which, one displays reliability in managing time, coordinating with others in a wide variety of cooperative situations, and manages materials and information. Discipline also reflects the motivation of the student, i.e. the attitude with which work is tackled. Does the student reveal a clear intention to give her or his best effort to address the challenges presented by the work under consideration? (Program)

5 Ethical Reasoning

Broadly speaking, Ethical Reasoning refers to all deliberation that concerns what individuals and groups should do, how they ought to act, and how we should treat one another. Literally, all human action, no matter how mundane or technical, falls within the ethical sphere. Excellence in Ethical Reasoning, then, requires (1) that we are keenly aware of the ethical dimension of all human action whether the action is individual or collective, and (2) that we can present and explain compelling arguments why we should or should not act in a certain way. (College)

6 Global Appreciation

Competency in Global Studies includes a set of knowledge and skills for living on a planet characterized by ever-closer economic, cultural, environmental interdependency. Through it, students will come to understand and take responsibility for their own and their nation’s role in global affairs.

7 Knowledge

This competency area reflects the amount of new information pertinent to the course demonstrably retained by the student as a result of listening to lectures, reading books and journals, and viewing and/or listening to all modes of media. (Program)

8 Oral Communication

Oral communication competence is the ability to use oral and nonverbal messages to convey ideas, information and intentions effectively in a manner that is appropriate to the topic, situation and audience. It is the ability to use listening skills to interpret accurately and critically the oral and nonverbal messages produced by others, and to display regard for other speakers’ points of view. Finally, it requires being able to select or develop a topic, research that topic, and organize and evaluate gathered information for presentational purposes. (College)

9 Quantitative Literacy

Quantitative literacy ensures the understanding and communication of rules of number, pattern manipulation and associated terminology for everyday applications. It allows the individual to use numbers and formulate relationships between them, to analyze, interpret and develop an appropriate strategy for meeting that individual’s current and future needs in employment and everyday life. (College)

10 Realization

Graduates must demonstrate the ability to apply technical skills —computer or handrendered — appropriately to problems and tasks specific to, and to the levels required of, their discipline. Appropriate usage includes the practice of responsible, ethical, and legal use of technology, information, and software resources. (Program)

11 Writing

In order to demonstrate mastery of the writing outcomes, the student’s writing should be clear, coherent, concise, comprehensive, creative and correct in a manner appropriate to a specific audience and purpose. (College)
